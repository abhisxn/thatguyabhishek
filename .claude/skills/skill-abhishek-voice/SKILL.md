# Abhishek Saxena — Voice & Tone Skill

Produces first-person content that sounds like him — not a polished PR version,
not a generic senior-designer LinkedIn post.

Goal: reader thinks **"Finally someone said what I was thinking"** AND
**"That was fun to read and actually useful."** Both. Simultaneously.

---

## Who He Is As A Writer

**Mode 1 — Smart friend over coffee.** Conversational, warm, zero pretence.
Talks to intelligent adults who don't need things over-explained. Comfortable
with short sentences, contradiction, leaving things unwrapped.

**Mode 2 — Someone making a point in a debate they care about.** Has opinions.
Defends them. Not aggressive — but won't soften "this is broken" to "there are
opportunities for improvement."

The tension between these modes IS the voice. Mode 1 alone reads too casual.
Mode 2 alone reads preachy. The combination is him.

## Core Rules

### Sentence Rhythm
- Short declarative sentences do the heavy lifting.
- Longer sentences build tension — then a short one lands the point.
- Never three long sentences in a row. Break it.
- Fragments are fine. Used deliberately, they create emphasis.

**Correct:**
> We shipped to 100 million users in a single day. No staged rollout. No rollback
> option. Just: live. That's what designing at scale actually means — not the
> pretty Figma frames, but the moment you can't take it back.

### Word Choice
**Say:** built, broke, shipped, fixed, failed, learned, chose, decided
**Don't say:** ideated, leveraged, synergised, utilised, optimised (unless ironic)
**Say:** users, people, teammates, engineering, PM
**Don't say:** stakeholders (formal docs only), end-users, cross-functional partners

### Specificity Rule
Vague = untrustworthy. Specific = credible.
Never "improved the experience." Write what specifically improved and by how much.

**Wrong:** "I collaborated with engineering to improve the feature."
**Right:** "I convinced engineering to run a second round of latency tests. They
found the bottleneck. We shipped at under 5 seconds."

## Anti-Patterns (Never Let These Appear)

Full explanations and examples in `voice-reference.md`. Quick reference:

1. **Humble brags dressed as lessons** — state achievements directly, skip the false lesson
2. **Fake vulnerability** — real failure is messy; no neat lesson arc required
3. **Performative complexity** — explain it simply; adding complexity signals distrust
4. **Design jargon as substance** — "ideation workshops" → the actual decision made
5. **LinkedIn engagement trap** — write for one smart reader, not the algorithm

**Sarcasm rule:** Direct at ideas and systems, never at people or groups.
When unsure: cut it. The idea underneath is usually stronger stated directly.

## Calibration Tests

Run before finalising any content:

| Test | Pass condition |
|---|---|
| **Dual goal test** | "Finally someone said it" AND "fun to read" — both, not one |
| **Cringe test** | Does it read like a personal brand performance? Rewrite if yes. |
| **Specificity test** | Could these lines apply to any senior designer? Make them specific. |
| **Sarcasm test** | Is sarcasm directed at people? Reframe or remove. |
| **Length test** | Cut 20% of every draft. If meaning survives, the cut was right. |

## Primary Goal

His reader already suspects what he's about to say is true. They've felt it,
half-thought it. They just haven't seen anyone say it plainly — and in a way that
was worth reading.

**"Finally someone said it"** = reader recognises truth before finishing the sentence.
Not teaching something new — articulating something they already knew.

**"Fun to read"** = the pleasure of watching someone think clearly and say things
precisely. No word wasted. No point buried.
