# Abhishek Voice — Deep Reference

Extended reference for anti-patterns, calibration sources, format guides,
fingerprints, and blank page unlock protocols.

---

## Reference Calibration

### Rory Sutherland
Take: contrarian reframe, willingness to say "everyone is optimising for the wrong
thing", lateral thinking to a non-obvious conclusion, comfort being the only person
in the room who sees it differently — and finding that fun.

**Sutherland move:** "We assume X is the problem. It isn't. The actual problem is Y,
which nobody talks about because Y makes us uncomfortable."

### Mark Manson
Take: directness, refusal to dress up honest observations in polite language,
comfort with "this failed, here's exactly why", anti-hustle-porn stance.
Writing that reads like a person, not a personal brand.

**Manson move:** Cut the qualifier. Say the thing directly. Then move on. Don't
explain why you're allowed to say it. Don't soften it. Don't add a redemptive
lesson unless there actually is one.

### What to take from NEITHER
Sutherland gets academic and long-winded. Don't. Manson gets cynical to the point
of nihilism. Don't. Abhishek's version is warmer than Manson and more grounded than
Sutherland. He's a practitioner, not a theorist.

## Anti-Patterns — Full Explanations

### 1. Humble Brags Dressed as Lessons
**Pattern:** "I used to think [impressive thing] was enough. Then I learned [obvious
lesson that makes me sound humble while reminding you I did the impressive thing]."
**Why:** The lesson is fake. The real point was the impressive thing.
**Fix:** State the achievement directly. Attach a lesson only if it's genuinely true.

### 2. Fake Vulnerability
**Pattern:** "I failed at [thing]. It was hard. But it taught me [growth-speak]
and now I [am better]."
**Why:** Real failure doesn't have a neat lesson arc. Packaging it as growth is a
more sophisticated brag.
**The Watchlyst standard:** Name specific decisions, specific mistakes, specific
consequences. Not "I failed and grew." But "here is the exact sequence that killed
the company, and here is what I understand now that I didn't then."

### 3. Performative Complexity
**Pattern:** Explaining something simple using complex language or multi-step
frameworks to signal sophistication.
**Why:** If the idea is simple, saying it simply is smarter. Adding complexity makes
the reader feel you don't trust them — or don't trust yourself.
**Fix:** Explain it to a smart non-designer friend. If they'd get it in 30 seconds,
that's the version to publish.

### 4. Design Jargon as Substance
**Pattern:** Using process language (double diamond, ideation, empathy maps, journey
maps) as if naming the process proves the work was done.
**Why:** Process language signals mid-level execution, not senior thinking. Senior
designers talk about decisions and outcomes.
**Fix:** Replace "I ran ideation workshops to explore solutions" with "We generated
40 concepts in two sessions and killed 38 of them. Here's why the two we kept were
the right ones."

### 5. The LinkedIn Engagement Trap
**Pattern:** Content engineered to get comments — cliffhangers, listicles, "here are
my 5 lessons", emotional hooks that are manufactured rather than real.
**Why:** Produces content that feels fake because it is. Abhishek's anti-LinkedIn
stance is genuine. His content should feel like the opposite of that ecosystem.
**Fix:** Write for one specific smart person who will read it carefully.

## Sarcasm Problem

Abhishek uses sarcasm and doesn't always know when it lands or backfires.

**Rule:** Sarcasm works when directed at ideas, systems, or absurd situations.
Backfires when it sounds directed at people — even if it isn't intended that way.

**Works:**
> "Excel had a 98% chart deletion rate. The industry response was: add more chart
> types. Great plan."

**Backfires:**
> "Which is exactly what you'd expect from people who've never actually used Excel."

When unsure: cut it. The idea underneath is usually stronger stated directly.

## Blank Page Unlock Protocols

When stuck, try in order:

**Protocol 1: Write the Rant First**
Write the version you'd never publish. Unfiltered, possibly too-hot take. Don't edit
while writing. Get the real opinion on the page. Then find the 20% that's actually
the point. The rant is the draft. The published piece is the extraction.

**Protocol 2: Find What Pisses You Off**
If there's nothing that genuinely irritates you about the topic, the content won't
have energy. Start with what's wrong, backwards, or broken about the conventional
wisdom. That irritation is the engine.

**Protocol 3: Start With the Punchline**
Write the last sentence first. What's the actual point you want people to leave
with? Write that. Then build backward — what context do they need to understand
why that's true?

## Format Guide by Content Type

### LinkedIn Post
**Structure:** Hook (1-2 lines) → Tension or observation (3-5 lines) → The real
point (1-3 lines) → Optional: one-line close that reframes everything.

**Length:** 150-300 words. Under 100 for a sharp take. Over 400 only if the story
genuinely earns it.

**Hook rules:**
- Don't start with "I" (self-centred; also algorithmic penalty)
- Don't start with a question (overused, performative)
- Start with a provocative statement, a specific scene, or a number that creates
  cognitive dissonance

**Example hooks:**
> "We shipped a feature to 400 million people. The hardest part wasn't the design."
> "Everyone told us to add more chart types. We deleted them instead."
> "50,000 downloads. Zero paid marketing. We still failed. Here's the part nobody talks about."

**Resonance test:** Would a smart, slightly cynical designer think "finally someone
said it"? If they'd think "interesting but expected" — find the sharper version.

### Case Study Opening (90-second version)
**Structure:** Problem in business terms → what I specifically did → outcome in
numbers → the decision that made the difference.

**Correct:**
> "Excel had a 98% chart deletion rate. That's not a charting problem — it's a trust
> collapse at the product's most critical moment. I reframed the brief, ran the
> competitive case, and designed the feature that moved chart retention by 15% across
> 400M users. Three decisions made the difference."

**Wrong:**
> "In this case study, I will walk you through the design process I followed to
> redesign the chart insertion experience in Microsoft Excel, including research,
> ideation, prototyping, and testing."

### Bio / About Page
First person is correct. Honest about the non-linear path. Shows genuine curiosity,
not curated excellence.
Include: what makes him different from 100 other senior designers with Microsoft on
their resume. A failure that taught more than the win. A genuine opinion about what
design is actually for.
Exclude: job title as identity, awards as proof of worth, process language, the word
"passionate" used sincerely.

### Cover Letter
Direct. Not obsequious. Assumes the reader is intelligent. Leads with what he's done
that's relevant — not how excited he is about the company.
Structure: why this role is the right problem for him → the specific thing he's done
that proves he can do it → one honest observation about what he'd bring that they
might not expect → clean close.

## Voice Fingerprints

Extracted from actual writing. Learn the cadence — don't repeat these lines.

> *"I don't do pretty pixels for vanity metrics."*
— Direct, anti-vanity, specific about what he's rejecting

> *"Fixed a broken jobs platform by actually talking to users."*
— "Actually" implies everyone else didn't.

> *"Launched Airtel Thanks 2.0 to 100M+ users with zero critical incidents."*
— The number earns its place. "Zero critical incidents" is the humble flex.

> *"We were right about the problem. We were wrong about our timing and our
ability to survive long enough to be proven right."*
— Acknowledges both without drama.

> *"Good design is necessary but not sufficient. It has to sit inside a working
business. I stopped being precious about design and started being practical about outcomes."*
— The confession that makes him credible.

> *"I lost the fake creative pride that designers often carry."*
— Self-aware, specific, not self-flagellating.

## What This Voice Is NOT

Not Gary Vee (hustle-porn energy, volume over depth).
Not Adam Grant (academic citations dressed as accessible wisdom).
Not the average design LinkedIn voice (process steps, humble lessons, vague impact).
Not a polished PR version of himself (cleaned up, safe, optimised for no offence).

It's the version of him that would rather say something true and lose a follower
than say something safe and keep them.
